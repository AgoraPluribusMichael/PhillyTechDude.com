On Extremism, Conspiratorial Thinking, and Vigilantism

Shortly after the 2024 election, a lone wolf vigilante murdered the CEO of a private health insurance company in broad daylight on the streets of New York City, which I strongly condemn, especially when there exist perfectly peaceful and legal ways to reform the health insurance industry. But this points to a broader phenomenon, where if the government and proper authorities don't do their job, then there is a risk for vigilantes to come out of the woodwork and pursue their own version of so-called "justice." In this way, I view vigilantism as a policy failure. But to dig down deeper, I truly believe that this form of vigilantism fits into a larger pattern: people risk being sucked into third-party political ideologies, bizarre conspiracy theories, or violence when they lose faith in society.

People who join third parties self-select for those who have lost faith in the two major parties. And while the policies and attitudes differ greatly among them, they share a common theme: the complete loss of faith in one major aspect of our society. Anarchists have lost faith in the government, ecosocialists have lost faith in the economy, and populists have lost faith in the establishment. And while third-party policies tend to be extreme, regressive, or unrealistic, they are at least grounded in a real criticism of the system and are constructive in nature, as they attempt to enact positive change through our existing political and legal democratic process. And it helps that they can commiserate together instead of completely isolating themselves away from the rest of the mainstream public. But it is still a stinging indictment of the Democratic and Republican parties for some people to be searching outside the mainstream for something to fix the problem, a symptom of incompetence and negligence.

During the pandemic, there was a huge spike in the prevalence of conspiracies. I think it has to do with this overwhelming and unbearable feeling of fear and helplessness. Everything shut down, and people were dying, and the Trump Administration so thoroughly bungled its response to the pandemic, that even some of Trump's most ardent supporters were calling it a "plandemic," as if it were entirely predictable that Trump would fail to respond competently that someone could have planned COVID-19 to sink his re-election campaign. In fact, my mental health took a dire turn at the time, and I started to go down the road of voluntaryist libertarianism and psychosis. Luckily for me, four years of a mixture of psychiatric care from Crozer Psychiatry and therapy across several mental health providers allowed me to make a full recovery. I hypothesize that people tend to look to conspiracy theories, because they find it more comforting to believe that someone is in control, that there is a network of shadowy, nefarious figures that have corrupted and taken control of the government and pull the strings behind the scenes to oppress the masses, which prevents even the most good-hearted and powerful politicians from addressing systemic injustices. For a widow grieving the loss of her husband to COVID-19, when they were supposed to retire in a few years and travel the world together, it's only human for her to be desperately searching for a reason, for someone to behind all this.

On the whole, this can form a negative feedback loop, as the extreme third-party policies and bizarre conspiracy theories can be so off-putting to the general public, that it can result in chronic isolation, delusion, and despair. For some who stew for long enough in a place of isolation and unbearable desperation, they eventually feel the need to lash out through violent individual action, either directed outwardly (mass shootings, murders, acts of terror) or inwardly (addiction, self harm, suicide, deaths of despair) â€” the tragic and natural outcome of a complete loss of faith in any institution, system, or legal means to achieve justice. This is my assessment for how someone who was previously a happy and productive member of society could end up murdering a health insurance CEO on the streets of New York City.

In summary, I view extremism, despair, cynicism, isolation, and vigilante violence as symptoms of existential societal rot, which paves the way for oppressive "strong-man" authoritarianism to fill the void where everyone else has failed. This is why I believe that the widespread loss of faith in our society is the biggest policy failure of our times.

The best way I can think of to combat this trend is to inspire people, on an individual level, to realize their capabilities to make a positive change through peaceful and legal means. Together, we can leverage existing power structures and legal procedures to be the change we want to see.
